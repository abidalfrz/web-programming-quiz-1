Top 3 Must Try

Tahu Takwa

Tahu Takwa is the undisputed culinary icon of Kediri, a local delicacy instantly recognizable by its vibrant, sunny yellow color. Unlike ordinary tofu, Tahu Takwa boasts a remarkably solid, dense, and satisfyingly chewy texture, a result of a special pressing process using high-quality soybeans. Its signature color and savory taste come from being soaked in a natural brine of turmeric and salt, which not only seasons the tofu to its core but also acts as a natural preservative. Typically enjoyed fried to a perfect golden-brown crisp on the outside while remaining warm and tender inside, it is often served hot with a side of rich petis (a sweet and savory shrimp paste sauce) and fresh bird's eye chilies.

Nasi Tumpang

Nasi Tumpang is a truly classic and soulful dish from Kediri, offering a unique and robust taste of Javanese heritage. At its heart is the remarkable sambal tumpang, a thick, creamy, and spicy sauce that sets it apart from any other rice dish. The sambal's unmistakable pungent aroma and deeply savory flavor come from its core ingredient: tempeh semangit, which is tempeh that has been left to over-ferment, unlocking a powerful umami character. This special tempeh is then cooked down with a rich blend of coconut milk, kaffir lime leaves, galangal, and other spices. The dish itself is simple yet satisfying: a scoop of warm rice is generously ladled with this potent sambal and traditionally served with an assortment of boiled vegetables (kuluban) like spinach, long beans, and bean sprouts, which provide a fresh, earthy balance. Often topped with a crispy rempeyek (peanut cracker) for a delightful crunch.

Sate Bekicot

Sate bekicot is a unique and adventurous culinary specialty from the Kediri region. This dish is made from bekicot, or land snails, which undergo a meticulous preparation process to transform them into a savory delicacy. First, the snails are thoroughly cleaned and boiled with aromatic herbs like guava leaves and lemongrass to eliminate any earthy smell and ensure a pleasant, non-slimy texture. The cooked snail meat is then marinated in a rich and flavorful Javanese spice blend (bumbu) dominated by sweet soy sauce, shallots, and garlic before being threaded onto bamboo skewers. Unlike most satays, Sate Bekicot is typically not grilled over charcoal but is served ready-to-eat after the marination, generously drizzled with a spicy peanut sauce or a special seasoning paste.
